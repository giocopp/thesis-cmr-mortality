RODRÍGUEZ SÁNCHEZ, WUCHERPFENNIG, RISCHKE, IACUS (2023)
Search-and-Rescue in the Central Mediterranean Route Does Not Induce Migration:
Predictive Modeling to Answer Causal Queries in Migration Research
STUDY SHEET (WITH IDENTIFICATION CRITIQUE)

==================================================
1) INSTITUTIONAL SETTING — QUICK
==================================================

- The paper studies the Central Mediterranean Route (CMR), the most frequented and deadly
  irregular migration route toward Europe during 2010–2020.

- Three distinct intervention periods are identified based on changes in SAR politics:

  1. State-led SAR (Mare Nostrum): October 18, 2013 – October 31, 2014.
     - The largest state-led SAR operation in European history.
     - Also had an anti-smuggling component: targeted and criminalized smugglers,
       destroyed wooden vessels used for transport.

  2. Private-led SAR (NGOs): from August 26, 2014 onward.
     - Began with MOAS, then many other NGOs joined.
     - EU operations (e.g., Triton) were simultaneously in place but operated farther
       from the Libyan coast and with a smaller area.
     - After 2015, rescue operations shifted closer to Libya.

  3. EU–Libya cooperation (pushbacks): from January 1, 2017 onward.
     - Extension of the Libyan SAR maritime zone.
     - Increasing involvement of the Libyan Coast Guard (LCG) in coordinated pushbacks.
     - Shift from saving migrants to deterrence and externalization of border control.
     - Linked to criminalization of NGOs and the "pull factor" discourse.

- The "pull factor" claim:
  The paper directly tests three related assertions:
  (1) SAR boats encourage more crossing attempts;
  (2) SAR unintentionally helps smugglers by reducing their operational costs;
  (3) therefore SAR makes the trip more dangerous.

- Main substantive conclusion:
  The paper finds no empirical support for SAR as a pull factor.
  Crossing attempts during SAR periods can be fully explained by other push-and-pull factors.
  Only the pushback/LCG intervention period shows a measurable reduction in crossings.

==================================================
2) DATA AND DESCRIPTIVE EVIDENCE
==================================================

MAIN DATA

- Outcome variable: monthly crossing attempts on the CMR, 2010–2020.
  Crossing attempts = arrivals (to Italy/Malta) + pushbacks (to Libya/Tunisia) + deaths in transit.
  This is broader than just arrivals — it captures the full volume of attempted crossings.

- Sources for the outcome:
  - Arrivals: Frontex illegal border crossings data.
  - Pushbacks: Tunisian and Libyan Coast Guard reports.
  - Deaths: compiled from UNITED for Intercultural Action, Migrants File,
    and IOM Missing Migrants Project.

- Predictive covariates (push-and-pull factors):
  A rich set of exogenous time series used to build the counterfactual, including:
  - Conflict intensity: ACLED data for African countries.
  - Commodity prices: IMF data for 30+ commodities (energy, agriculture, metals).
  - Currency exchange rates: African/MENA currencies vs. Euro (ECB).
  - Labor market indicators: EU unemployment (Eurostat); Google Trends for "job"/"work"
    in Arabic for North African countries (proxy for African unemployment).
  - Weather: temperature, precipitation, storms in Malta and Italy.
  - Environmental disasters: natural and technological disasters in Africa.
  - Air traffic: passenger flows from Africa/MENA to Europe (Sabre data),
    used to capture unobserved factors affecting mobility.
  - Syrian conflict intensity: Google Trends searches.
  - All covariates lagged up to 6 months.

- Covariate selection:
  A spike-and-slab prior embedded in the model selects which covariates
  are most predictive of the target series, automatically penalizing non-predictive ones.

DESCRIPTIVE PATTERNS

- SAR periods coincide with higher crossing attempts and lower mortality rates.
- The pre-SAR period (2010–2013) and the post-SAR/pushback period show higher
  and more volatile mortality rates.
- Key point: SAR is not associated with higher mortality — if anything, mortality is lower
  during SAR. The absence of SAR and the start of pushbacks are associated with higher mortality.

IMPORTANT LIMITATIONS

- Deaths are likely underreported (estimated ~10% unrecorded).
  However, deaths are only ~4% of total crossing attempts, so underreporting
  is unlikely to drive the main time-series behavior.
- Pushback data from Libya/Tunisia may also be measured with error.
- Monthly aggregation prevents detection of within-month feedback mechanisms
  (e.g., smugglers reacting to SAR presence within days).

==================================================
3) METHODOLOGY
==================================================

CORE APPROACH: Bayesian Structural Time-Series (BSTS) model.

The idea:
- Use a rich set of exogenous push-and-pull factors to predict what crossing attempts
  would have looked like in the absence of each intervention.
- Train the model on the pre-intervention period.
- Compare the predicted counterfactual with the observed series in the post-intervention period.
- If there is no significant difference, the intervention did not affect crossings.
- If the observed series is significantly below the prediction, the intervention reduced crossings.

Why BSTS and not alternatives:
- The crossing-attempts series has trends, seasonality, non-stationarity,
  and high autocorrelation — all at once.
- Standard difference-in-differences assumes no autocorrelation → inappropriate.
- Synthetic control requires control time series of similar units → unavailable here
  (there is no comparable untreated migration route).
- BSTS can handle all these features simultaneously by combining:
  - a local linear trend component,
  - a seasonal component,
  - a regression component with exogenous covariates,
  - spike-and-slab variable selection.

Technical structure:
- Observation equation: Y_t = Z_t^T α_t + ε_t
  (links observed crossings to latent state)
- State equation: α_{t+1} = T_t α_t + R_t η_t
  (governs how the state evolves over time)
- Estimation via MCMC (10,000 iterations).
- The model is fit separately for each of the three intervention periods,
  each time using all pre-intervention data to learn the dynamics.

Key identification assumption:
- The control time series (covariates) must be exogenous to the SAR interventions.
  That is, conflicts, commodity prices, weather, air traffic, etc., must not be caused
  by changes in SAR policy.
- The authors test this via Granger causality tests and find no evidence of violation.
- They also show results are robust to excluding labor market indicators
  (the only covariates that could conceivably be mediators).

What the model produces:
- A predicted counterfactual time series with 95% confidence intervals.
- "Pointwise effects": the difference between observed and predicted at each time point.
- If the observed series falls within the confidence intervals, no significant effect.

==================================================
4) RESULTS
==================================================

A. MARE NOSTRUM (STATE-LED SAR)

- The observed crossing attempts after October 2013 remain within the confidence intervals
  of the predicted counterfactual.
- A slight bump in Spring-Summer 2014 (observed slightly above predicted),
  but the difference is not statistically significant.
- Conclusion: Mare Nostrum did not significantly increase crossing attempts
  beyond what push-and-pull factors alone would predict.

B. NGO-LED SAR (PRIVATE-LED SAR)

- This is described as "a more straightforward test" of the pull factor claim.
- The observed and predicted counterfactual series follow each other closely
  for nearly two years, including during the months of most intense NGO activity.
- No discernible difference in seasonality either.
- If anything, by mid-2017, the model predicts the observed flow should have been
  higher than what was actually observed — the opposite of the pull factor prediction.
  (Though this difference is also not significant.)
- Conclusion: no support for SAR as a pull factor.

C. EU–LIBYA COOPERATION (PUSHBACKS)

- Here, the predicted counterfactual is substantially higher than the observed series.
- The model predicts that, based on push-and-pull factors, crossing attempts should have
  been much higher than what was actually observed after the LCG took over.
- This difference is statistically significant.
- Conclusion: the pushback/externalization policy did reduce crossing attempts.
- But the authors note this came at the cost of well-documented human rights violations
  in Libyan detention centers and during pushback operations.

D. MORTALITY PATTERNS

- During SAR periods, the mortality rate per crossing attempt was lower and less volatile.
- During the pushback period, the mortality rate increased.
- SAR is associated with saving lives, not with making crossings more dangerous.
- Caveat: mortality data is subject to underreporting.

==================================================
5) IDENTIFICATION CRITIQUE / HOW CONVINCING IS THE CAUSAL CLAIM?
==================================================

CORE STRENGTHS

1. The method is well-suited to the data.
   - BSTS handles the key time-series features (trend, seasonality, autocorrelation)
     that other methods ignore or mishandle.
   - Previous studies used naive before/after comparisons or simple regressions
     that did not account for serial correlation — a serious methodological flaw.

2. The counterfactual is built from plausibly exogenous factors.
   - Conflicts, commodity prices, weather, air traffic, etc., are unlikely to be caused
     by SAR policy changes.
   - Granger causality tests support the exogeneity assumption.

3. The model accurately predicts the pre-intervention series.
   - Good pre-intervention fit is the best validation of the counterfactual.
   - The observed series generally falls within narrow confidence intervals.

4. The approach can detect effects when they exist.
   - The significant result for the pushback period shows the method has power
     to detect real policy effects — lending credibility to the null results for SAR.

5. The outcome is crossing attempts (arrivals + pushbacks + deaths), not just arrivals.
   - This is a better measure of the actual flow than arrivals alone.

CORE CONCERNS

1. Monthly aggregation is coarse.
   - Feedback mechanisms between SAR and smuggler behavior may operate
     at daily or weekly frequency. Monthly data cannot detect these.
   - Deiana et al. (2024) use daily data and find SAR effects on boat composition
     and weather sensitivity — effects that are invisible at monthly frequency.

2. The null result does not distinguish between "no effect on total crossings"
   and "no effect on behavior."
   - Even if total crossings are explained by push-and-pull factors,
     SAR could still change how crossings happen (boat type, timing, route).
   - This paper operates at the aggregate level and explicitly refrains from
     claims about micro-level behavior — but the pull factor debate is partly
     about behavioral mechanisms, not just aggregate counts.

3. The method asks a specific question: did SAR change the level of crossings?
   - It does not ask whether SAR changed the composition or riskiness of crossings.
   - Deiana et al. show SAR changed the weather elasticity and boat composition
     without necessarily changing the total level — these are compatible findings.

4. Post-intervention periods overlap with later interventions.
   - The authors acknowledge they cannot identify delayed effects in later intervals
     when another intervention was already in place.
   - The Mare Nostrum prediction becomes less reliable over longer horizons
     (wider confidence intervals).

5. Predictions degrade over time.
   - The model predicts well for about two years, then uncertainty grows.
   - This limits the ability to make claims about long-run effects.

6. The paper explicitly critiques the market-based approach of Deiana et al.
   - It argues that micro-founded models make untestable assumptions about
     individual behavior (prices, boat choice, risk perception) using aggregate data.
   - It calls this an "ecological fallacy."
   - However, this critique cuts both ways: the BSTS approach also uses aggregate data
     and cannot identify whether the aggregate null masks heterogeneous behavioral responses.

WHAT SUPPORTS THEIR INTERPRETATION

- The model fit in all three pre-intervention periods is good.
- The null result for SAR holds across both state-led and private-led SAR periods.
- The significant result for pushbacks shows the method can detect real effects.
- Descriptive evidence confirms SAR periods had lower mortality rates.
- Granger causality tests and robustness checks support covariate exogeneity.

WHAT STILL LIMITS THE PAPER

- Monthly frequency misses behavioral responses operating at higher frequency.
- The paper cannot speak to boat composition, weather sensitivity, or route changes.
- The null at the aggregate level does not rule out offsetting behavioral effects
  (e.g., more crossings on unsafe boats offset by fewer crossings on safe boats).
- The paper's strong normative framing ("pull factor claim lacks support")
  may overstate what a null result at aggregate monthly frequency can establish.

BEST CRITICAL SUMMARY

- The paper convincingly shows that aggregate monthly crossing attempts during SAR periods
  can be predicted by exogenous push-and-pull factors, with no residual effect attributable to SAR.
- This is an important contribution that directly challenges the pull factor narrative.
- However, it does not rule out that SAR changed the composition, timing, or riskiness
  of crossings — effects that other papers (Deiana et al.) identify at higher frequency.
- The two sets of findings are not necessarily contradictory: SAR may not change
  how many people attempt to cross, while still changing how they cross.

==================================================
6) CONNECTING METHODOLOGY TO RESULTS
==================================================

KEY DEEP LOGIC

- The paper's question is: can we explain the observed level of crossing attempts
  using only exogenous push-and-pull factors, without needing SAR as an explanation?
- If yes, SAR is not a pull factor at the aggregate level.
- If the predicted counterfactual diverges from the observed, the intervention had an effect.

HOW THE RESULTS FIT TOGETHER

- Panel A (Mare Nostrum): no divergence → SAR not a pull factor.
- Panel B (NGO SAR): no divergence → SAR not a pull factor.
- Panel C (pushbacks): significant divergence → pushbacks reduced crossings.
- Mortality descriptives: SAR periods had lower mortality, pushback periods higher.

THE STRONGEST CLAIM IS:

- At the aggregate monthly level, crossing attempts during SAR are fully explained
  by structural push-and-pull factors; there is no residual "pull" from SAR itself.
- Pushback policies do reduce aggregate crossings, but at a humanitarian cost.

THE CLAIM DOES NOT EXTEND TO:

- Whether SAR changed behavioral margins (boat type, weather sensitivity, route choice).
- Whether SAR affected individual-level decision-making of migrants or smugglers.
- Whether the composition of risk changed even if the level of crossings did not.

==================================================
7) RELATIONSHIP TO DEIANA ET AL. (2024)
==================================================

These two papers reach findings that appear contradictory but are actually compatible
once you understand what each paper identifies:

RODRÍGUEZ SÁNCHEZ ET AL.:
- Question: Did SAR increase the total number of crossing attempts?
- Method: BSTS counterfactual at monthly frequency.
- Answer: No. Aggregate crossings are explained by push-and-pull factors.
- Frequency: Monthly. Cannot detect within-month behavioral responses.

DEIANA ET AL.:
- Question: Did SAR change how crossings respond to weather and change boat composition?
- Method: Poisson model with daily wave-height interactions and week-by-year FE.
- Answer: Yes. SAR induced substitution toward inflatable boats and changed
  the weather elasticity of crossings — evidence of moral hazard.
- Frequency: Daily. Identifies behavioral margins invisible at monthly level.

WHY THEY ARE COMPATIBLE:
- SAR may not change the total volume of crossings (Rodríguez Sánchez et al.)
  while still changing the composition of crossings (Deiana et al.).
- If SAR shifts some crossings from safe to unsafe boats, the total count may be similar,
  but the average riskiness increases.
- Rodríguez Sánchez et al. explicitly acknowledge they cannot speak to micro-level behavior.
- Deiana et al. explicitly acknowledge they cannot estimate the total unconditional effect on crossings.

The two papers are best read as answering different questions about the same phenomenon.

==================================================
8) BOTTOM LINE
==================================================

- The paper provides strong evidence that SAR (both state-led and NGO-led)
  did not increase aggregate crossing attempts on the Central Mediterranean Route.
- Crossing volumes during SAR periods are well predicted by exogenous structural factors.
- Pushback policies (EU–Libya cooperation) did measurably reduce crossings,
  but at the cost of documented human rights violations.
- SAR periods coincide with lower, not higher, mortality rates.

MOST IMPORTANT TAKEAWAY

- The "pull factor" claim — that SAR itself drives more migration — lacks empirical support
  at the aggregate level.
- Migration flows are driven by structural push-and-pull factors (conflict, economics,
  environmental conditions), not by the availability of rescue at sea.

MAIN STRENGTH

- Innovative use of BSTS for causal inference in a time-series setting
  where standard methods (DiD, synthetic control) are inappropriate.
- The method can detect effects when they exist (as shown by the pushback result),
  lending credibility to the null results for SAR.

MAIN LIMITATION

- Monthly frequency cannot detect behavioral responses (boat switching,
  weather sensitivity) that operate at daily/weekly timescales.
- The aggregate null does not rule out important compositional changes
  in how crossings occur, which other studies have documented.
