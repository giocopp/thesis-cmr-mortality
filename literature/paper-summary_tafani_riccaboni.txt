TAFANI, RICCABONI (2025)
The Impact of the EU–Turkey Agreement on the Number of Lives Lost at Sea
STUDY SHEET (WITH IDENTIFICATION CRITIQUE)

==================================================
1) INSTITUTIONAL SETTING — QUICK
==================================================

- The paper studies the Mediterranean migration region as a whole, covering five routes:
  - Western Mediterranean (Algeria/Morocco to Spain)
  - Western African (West Africa to Canary Islands)
  - Central Mediterranean (Libya/Tunisia/Egypt to Italy/Malta)
  - Eastern Mediterranean (Turkey to Greece/Cyprus)
  - Eastern Border (Eastern Europe to Turkey by land)

- The key policy event is the EU–Turkey statement, formally agreed on March 18, 2016.
  - The agreement was a response to the 2015 migration crisis.
  - Turkey committed to take back all irregular migrants who entered Greece after March 20, 2016.
  - In return: EU pledged €6 billion in financial aid, visa liberalization, and a "1:1 mechanism"
    (for every person sent back, one Syrian refugee resettled from Turkey to the EU).
  - Non-Syrian nationalities (Afghans, Iraqis, Pakistanis) were left with limited access to protection.

- The paper's central concern:
  - The agreement effectively shut down the Eastern Mediterranean route.
  - But did it simply displace migrants to other, more dangerous routes — especially the Central Mediterranean?
  - If so, the agreement's apparent success on the Eastern route came at the cost of increased deaths elsewhere.

- Context:
  - 2016 saw the highest number of deaths (5,143) among migrants attempting to reach Europe.
  - This happened while arrivals fell by more than 60%.
  - The Central Mediterranean route is much longer and more dangerous than the Eastern Mediterranean route.
  - Migrants on the Central route travel an average of 153 days, versus 62 days on the Eastern route.

- Main substantive question:
  - Did the EU–Turkey deal generate a significant diversion effect from the Eastern to the Central route?
  - And did this diversion lead to additional deaths at sea?

==================================================
2) DATA AND DESCRIPTIVE EVIDENCE
==================================================

MAIN DATA

- Migration flows:
  - Monthly irregular border crossings from Frontex, 2011 to early 2017.
  - Disaggregated by nationality and by route.
  - Five routes covered.
  - Each observation is a nationality–route–month combination.
  - Total observations: 73,440 (without controls), 62,796 (with controls).

- Deaths / missing migrants:
  - From the IOM Missing Migrants Project.
  - Monthly fatalities and disappearances by route.
  - Available for four Mediterranean routes (Eastern Border excluded due to lack of data).
  - Used in the second-stage regression analysis.

- Covariates:
  - GDP per capita (from Kummu et al. 2024): proxy for economic conditions in origin countries.
  - Fragile State Index (Fund for Peace): proxy for political instability in origin countries.
  - ACLED conflict data: deaths from violence against civilians in departure countries
    (Libya, Morocco, Turkey), used in the regression on missing migrants.
  - Note: ACLED was excluded from the main MC analysis due to data availability problems,
    but was used in the second-stage route-level regression.

- Treatment assignment:
  - A nationality is "treated" if its median number of crossings on the Eastern Mediterranean route
    in the pre-treatment period was above the aggregate median of all nationalities on that route.
  - Syrians excluded from the main analysis because they had special privileges under the 1:1 rule.
  - Alternative thresholds used for robustness: first quartile and third quartile.

DESCRIPTIVE PATTERNS

- Before the agreement:
  - Treated nationalities used predominantly the Eastern Mediterranean route.
  - The Central route had lower flows from these nationalities.

- After the agreement (April 2016):
  - Clear shift visible for treated nationalities: the ratio of Central-to-Eastern flows increases sharply.
  - No comparable shift for non-treated nationalities.

- Deaths:
  - Central route mortality rate roughly doubled after the agreement.
  - IOM noted an unusual increase in winter 2016 crossings and deaths, attributed to the closure
    of the Eastern route.
  - Before the agreement: missing migrants on the Central route were mostly isolated small incidents.
  - After: a noticeable rise in large shipwrecks, more dispersed along the Libyan coast.

IMPORTANT LIMITATIONS

- Missing Migrants data likely underestimates deaths, especially on the Central route.
- Deaths before embarkation (human trafficking, exploitation in Libya) are not captured.
- Nationality of missing migrants is not available in the IOM data,
  so the second-stage analysis aggregates at the route level.

==================================================
3) METHODOLOGY
==================================================

The analysis has two stages.

FIRST STAGE: COUNTERFACTUAL ESTIMATION OF DIVERSION EFFECTS

Core idea:
- Estimate what migration flows would have looked like in the absence of the EU–Turkey agreement
  for treated nationalities across all routes.
- The difference between observed and counterfactual flows is the Average Treatment Effect
  on the Treated (ATT).

Method: Matrix Completion (MC) estimator (Athey et al. 2021).

How it works:
- Think of the data as a large matrix: rows are nationality–route pairs, columns are months.
- After the agreement (April 2016), the treated entries are "missing" — we don't observe
  what would have happened without the deal.
- MC fills in these missing entries by assuming the full matrix has a low-rank structure.
  Intuitively, the patterns across many nationalities and routes contain enough information
  to predict the missing counterfactual values.
- Formally, MC solves:
    L = argmin [ (1/|O|) Σ (Y_it - L_it)^2 + λ_L · norm(L) ]
  where O is the set of untreated observations, and the norm penalty (soft impute)
  prevents overfitting by gradually shrinking singular values.

Why MC over alternatives:
- Generalized Synthetic Control (SC) was tried but gave contradictory results
  because it was overly sensitive to the 2015 migration spike.
  SC relies on parallel trends, which may be violated here.
  When data was standardized, SC results matched MC, suggesting the issue was
  methodological sensitivity rather than fundamentally different conclusions.
- Interactive Fixed Effects (IFE) was also tried but produced more volatile estimates
  for the same reason: it uses "hard impute" (binary selection of singular values)
  rather than the smooth regularization of MC.
- MC's soft impute is more robust to spikes, outliers, and high-dimensional settings.

Two model specifications:
- Without covariates: uses only time fixed effects and unit (nationality–route) fixed effects.
- With covariates: adds GDP per capita and Fragile State Index as controls.
  This reduces observations from 73,440 to 62,796 due to data availability.

Treatment timing:
- Treatment starts April 2016.
- Treatment assignment computed using data up to October 2015,
  to avoid contamination from the Joint Action Plan announced that month.

SECOND STAGE: LINKING DIVERSION TO DEATHS

- After estimating the ATT (the extra migrants on each route due to the agreement),
  the paper runs a two-way fixed effects regression:

    MM_{i,t} = α_i + γ_{t-1} + β_1 crossings_{i,t-1} + β_2 ATT_{i,t-1}
               + β_3 political_fatalities_{i,t-1} + ε_{i,t-1}

  where:
  - MM_{i,t} = missing migrants on route i in month t
  - α_i = route fixed effects (absorb time-invariant route characteristics)
  - γ_{t-1} = monthly fixed effects (absorb common time shocks)
  - ATT_{i,t-1} = estimated diversion effect from Stage 1 (lagged one month)
  - crossings = total border crossings (lagged)
  - political_fatalities = deaths from violence against civilians in the departure country (lagged)

- Sample: 142 observations (4 routes × ~37 months), from 2015 to 2017.
- Standard errors: clustered by route and time.

Final quantification:
- Multiply the monthly ATT for the Central route by the corresponding monthly mortality rate
  to estimate deaths attributable to the agreement.

==================================================
4) RESULTS
==================================================

A. COUNTERFACTUAL ANALYSIS: DIVERSION EFFECTS

Main result (baseline model: median threshold, no controls):

  - Central Mediterranean: ATT = +198 per month (p = 0.0008)
  - Eastern Mediterranean: ATT = −66 per month (p = 0.08)
  - Western Mediterranean: ATT = +6 (p = 0.03)
  - Western African: ATT = +1 (not significant)
  - Eastern Border: ATT = +1 (not significant)
  - Overall effect: ATT = +11 (not significant)

INTERPRETATION

- The agreement diverted approximately 198 migrants per month from the Eastern route
  to the Central route.
- Summing the monthly ATTs from April to November 2016 gives a total diversion
  of about 2,090 additional migrants on the Central route.
- The Eastern route shows a decrease in crossings, as expected, though only marginally significant.
- The overall total effect across all routes is not significant, which is consistent with
  redistribution across routes rather than net reduction.
- Western routes were unaffected, likely due to geographical distance from the Eastern route.

WHY THE MODEST SIGNIFICANCE ON THE EASTERN ROUTE MATTERS

- The paper's own results show that the decrease on the Eastern route is only marginally
  significant (p = 0.08 to 0.13 depending on specification).
- This is somewhat puzzling: if the agreement sharply reduced Eastern crossings,
  why is the ATT not more cleanly significant?
- The authors chose the model without controls as the baseline because:
  (1) controls reduce observations by ~10,000,
  (2) yearly controls are temporally mismatched with monthly outcomes,
  (3) results with/without controls are similar anyway.

B. ROBUSTNESS CHECKS

- Results robust across three threshold definitions (first quartile, median, third quartile).
- As nationalities are progressively added to the treated group:
  - ATT for Central route decreases smoothly
  - ATT for Eastern route increases
  - Western routes remain stable
- Placebo tests (4-month and 6-month pre-treatment): model passes all tests
  except the F-test in the pre-trend check. The equivalence test (more robust to outliers) passes.
- Pre-treatment ATTs are nearly zero, supporting model validity.
- Results broadly consistent with Generalized SC and IFE when data is standardized.

C. HAZARD OF CROSSINGS: REGRESSION RESULTS

Table 2 results (two-way fixed effects, 142 observations, R² = 0.589):

  - Crossings:            β = 0.001  (SE = 0.000, t = 2.54, p = 0.012)
  - ATT (diversion):      β = 0.877  (SE = 0.298, t = 2.95, p = 0.004)
  - Political fatalities:  β = 0.315  (SE = 0.137, t = 2.30, p = 0.024)

INTERPRETATION

- There is a significant positive correlation between the diversion effect (ATT)
  and the number of missing migrants.
- Each additional diverted migrant is associated with roughly 0.877 additional deaths.
- Higher volumes of total crossings also correlate with more deaths.
- Political instability in departure countries is also positively associated with deaths.

D. QUANTIFICATION OF LIVES LOST

- Multiplying the significant monthly ATT for the Central route by the monthly mortality rate
  from April to December 2016 yields an estimate of 45 ± 3 additional deaths
  attributable to the EU–Turkey agreement.

- The authors acknowledge this number has "symbolic weight" —
  the key point is the existence and direction of the effect, not the precise magnitude.

==================================================
5) IDENTIFICATION CRITIQUE / HOW CONVINCING IS THE CAUSAL CLAIM?
==================================================

CORE STRENGTHS

- The paper uses a serious causal inference method (Matrix Completion) rather than
  simple before/after or descriptive comparisons.
  This is a genuine contribution, since prior work on this agreement was almost entirely
  descriptive or legal/theoretical.

- MC is well-suited for this setting:
  - Many nationality–route units, relatively few time periods.
  - The low-rank assumption is more flexible than parallel trends.
  - Soft impute handles the 2015 migration spike better than SC or IFE.

- The treatment is plausibly exogenous to individual nationality–route pairs:
  the agreement was a high-level EU–Turkey political deal, not driven by the behavior
  of specific nationalities on specific routes.

- Multiple robustness checks support the main finding:
  different thresholds, different estimators, placebo tests, pre-trend tests.

CORE CONCERNS

1. The two-stage structure introduces generated-regressor bias.
   - The ATT from Stage 1 is an estimated quantity, not observed.
   - Using it as a regressor in Stage 2 may understate standard errors.
   - The paper does not discuss or correct for this.

2. The second-stage regression is very small (142 observations, 4 routes, ~37 months).
   - With route and time fixed effects, the effective degrees of freedom are quite limited.
   - Clustering on 4 routes is extremely sparse for cluster-robust inference.
   - The R² of 0.589 includes fixed effects; within R² is only 0.161,
     meaning most of the explanatory power comes from fixed effects, not the regressors.

3. The link from diversion to deaths is correlational, not cleanly causal.
   - The paper calls it a "correlation" and a "relationship," which is honest.
   - But the ATT itself is endogenously determined by the model in Stage 1.
   - Political fatalities are a rough proxy for departure-country conditions.
   - Omitted variables at the route-month level (weather, smuggler behavior,
     Libyan Coast Guard activity, NGO presence) could confound the regression.

4. Exclusion of Syrians is necessary but consequential.
   - Syrians were the largest group on the Eastern route.
   - Their exclusion means the analysis captures only the diversion of non-Syrian nationalities.
   - The total diversion effect (including Syrians who may have faced different incentives)
     could look quite different.

5. Treatment assignment is based on pre-treatment route usage.
   - This is reasonable but somewhat mechanical.
   - Nationalities near the threshold could be misclassified.
   - The iterative robustness check (progressively moving nationalities) helps,
     but the fundamental arbitrariness remains.

6. The mortality quantification (45 ± 3 deaths) is approximate.
   - It multiplies the estimated ATT by the observed mortality rate.
   - But the mortality rate is itself a noisy estimate (underreported deaths, uncertain denominator).
   - The ± 3 uncertainty band reflects only the ATT uncertainty, not the mortality rate uncertainty.
   - This number is best understood as a lower bound, as the authors note.

7. Pre-trend concern.
   - The model fails the F-test in the pre-trend test for the Central route.
   - The authors argue the equivalence test (which it passes) is more robust to outliers.
   - This is a reasonable argument but not completely reassuring.
   - If there were pre-existing trends in Central route flows for treated nationalities,
     the ATT could be biased upward.

8. Temporal scope.
   - The analysis ends in early 2017, only about 9 months after the agreement.
   - Longer-run effects (adaptation by smugglers, policy changes, new routes) are not captured.
   - The authors acknowledge this as a limitation.

WHAT SUPPORTS THEIR INTERPRETATION

- The diversion pattern is visible descriptively: treated nationalities shift from Eastern to Central.
- Non-treated nationalities show no comparable shift — a useful placebo-like check.
- The Central route ATT is consistently positive and significant across all specifications.
- Field evidence from Galos et al. (2017) confirms that the Central route is much more dangerous
  (longer journeys, more exploitation, more secondary migration).
- The IOM itself noted the unusual increase in late-2016 Central route crossings.

WHAT STILL LIMITS THE PAPER

- Only one major policy shock (the agreement), making it hard to rule out coincident confounders.
- The 2015 crisis was a massive structural break; any method struggles with such disruption.
- No direct observation of smuggler behavior, route choices, or prices.
- The paper cannot distinguish between migrants who genuinely diverted from the Eastern route
  and migrants who would have attempted the Central route regardless.
- Libyan civil war dynamics (intensifying throughout 2016) could independently
  increase both Central route flows and Central route deaths.

BEST CRITICAL SUMMARY

- The paper provides the first rigorous quantitative estimate of the diversion effect
  of the EU–Turkey agreement.
- The first-stage result (diversion to the Central route) is the strongest and most convincing finding.
- The second-stage result (link to deaths) is suggestive and directionally plausible
  but rests on a small sample and correlational design.
- The quantification of 45 deaths is best read as an order-of-magnitude illustration,
  not a precise causal estimate of mortality.

==================================================
6) CONNECTING METHODOLOGY TO RESULTS
==================================================

KEY DEEP LOGIC

- The paper's main contribution is methodological:
  applying Matrix Completion to the migration-policy context
  to construct a proper counterfactual for treated nationalities.

- Previous analyses simply compared totals before and after the agreement
  and concluded there was no significant diversion.
  The paper shows this conclusion is wrong: when you properly account for
  which nationalities were affected, a clear diversion appears.

HOW THE RESULTS FIT TOGETHER

- Stage 1 (MC estimation):
  Identifies the nationalities most affected by the agreement,
  constructs counterfactuals, and measures the monthly diversion to the Central route.

- Stage 2 (two-way FE regression):
  Tests whether the estimated diversion correlates with increased deaths,
  controlling for total flow volume and political instability.

- Descriptive evidence:
  Provides visual support (Figures 2, 3) for the diversion pattern
  and the increased mortality on the Central route after April 2016.

- Mortality quantification:
  Translates the statistical result into a concrete (if approximate) human cost.

SO THE STRONGEST CLAIM IS NOT:

- "We precisely measured 45 deaths caused by the EU–Turkey agreement."

THE STRONGEST CLAIM IS:

- The EU–Turkey agreement caused a statistically significant diversion of migrants
  from the Eastern Mediterranean to the more dangerous Central Mediterranean route,
  and this diversion is positively correlated with increased deaths at sea.

==================================================
7) CONNECTION TO DEIANA ET AL. (2024)
==================================================

Both papers study unintended consequences of migration policies in the Mediterranean,
but they focus on different mechanisms and different policies:

DEIANA ET AL.:
- Studies SAR operations on the Central Mediterranean route.
- Mechanism: moral hazard — rescue operations made inflatable boats viable,
  leading smugglers to substitute toward cheaper, more dangerous vessels.
- Identification: exploits daily variation in wave height interacted with SAR regimes
  and boat-type composition. The key is how weather sensitivity of crossings
  changes across SAR regimes.
- Evidence is strongest on behavioral mechanism (boat switching, weather elasticity).

TAFANI & RICCABONI:
- Studies the EU–Turkey agreement and its effect across Mediterranean routes.
- Mechanism: route diversion — shutting down the Eastern route pushed migrants
  to the more dangerous Central route.
- Identification: Matrix Completion counterfactual for treated nationalities,
  exploiting the cross-sectional structure of many nationality–route pairs.
- Evidence is strongest on the existence and magnitude of the diversion effect.

COMPLEMENTARY INSIGHTS:
- Together, the papers paint a picture where both supply-side interventions (SAR)
  and demand-side interventions (bilateral agreements) generate unintended consequences.
- Deiana et al. show that rescue operations change smuggler behavior within a route.
- Tafani & Riccaboni show that border agreements shift migrants across routes.
- Both conclude that narrowly targeted policies can backfire when migrants and smugglers
  respond strategically.

KEY METHODOLOGICAL CONTRAST:
- Deiana et al. use high-frequency (daily) variation in an exogenous variable (waves)
  to identify behavioral responses. This is closer to a quasi-experimental design.
- Tafani & Riccaboni use a panel counterfactual method (MC) with a single treatment event.
  This is methodologically sophisticated but relies more heavily on modeling assumptions.
- Deiana et al. cannot directly estimate total deaths caused by SAR.
- Tafani & Riccaboni attempt to quantify deaths but through a correlational second stage.

==================================================
8) BOTTOM LINE
==================================================

- The EU–Turkey agreement successfully reduced crossings on the Eastern Mediterranean route.
- But it generated a significant diversion effect: roughly 2,000 additional migrants
  were pushed to the Central Mediterranean route from April to December 2016.
- The Central route is far more dangerous (longer journeys, more exploitation, higher mortality).
- The paper estimates approximately 45 additional deaths at sea attributable to this diversion.

MOST IMPORTANT TAKEAWAY

- Bilateral migration agreements that focus on a single route are shortsighted.
- Migration patterns across routes are interdependent.
- Closing one route without addressing root causes or providing legal alternatives
  simply displaces the problem — and the danger — elsewhere.

MAIN STRENGTH

- First rigorous causal estimate of the diversion effect of the EU–Turkey agreement,
  using a modern counterfactual method suited to the data structure.
- Clear policy relevance: the EU continues to negotiate similar agreements
  (e.g., with Egypt in 2024), making these findings timely.

MAIN LIMITATION

- The link from diversion to deaths rests on a small-sample correlational regression,
  not a clean causal design.
- The analysis captures only a short post-treatment window (~9 months).
- The quantification of deaths is approximate and likely a lower bound.
