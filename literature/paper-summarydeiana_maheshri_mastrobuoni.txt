DEIANA, MAHESHRI, MASTROBUONI (2024)
Migrants at Sea: Unintended Consequences of Search-and-Rescue Operations
STUDY SHEET (WITH IDENTIFICATION CRITIQUE)

==================================================
1) INSTITUTIONAL SETTING — QUICK
==================================================

- The paper studies the Central Mediterranean route to Italy, mainly from Libya and Tunisia.
- It focuses on how different search-and-rescue (SAR) regimes changed migrant and smuggler behavior.
- Main policy sequence:
  - Hermes: limited baseline
  - Mare Nostrum: first very extensive SAR operation
  - Triton I
  - Triton II
  - Themis
- NGOs also became active near the Libyan coast from 2014 onward.

- A second crucial institutional shock is the Minniti Code of Conduct on August 7, 2017.
  - This made it harder for NGOs to operate close to Libya.
  - The paper treats this as a sharp reduction in rescue availability near the Libyan coast.

- Main substantive question:
  - Does SAR simply save people?
  - Or does it also generate moral hazard, by encouraging more crossings and more use of unsafe inflatable rafts, partly offsetting the intended safety gains?

==================================================
2) DATA AND DESCRIPTIVE EVIDENCE
==================================================

MAIN DATA

- Arrivals / crossings:
  - Daily irregular arrivals to Italy from the Polizia di Stato.
  - Main outcome: attempted crossings = arrivals + deaths in transit.
  - Average attempted crossings are about 170 per day.
  - Strong seasonality.

- Deaths:
  - Built by cross-referencing UNITED, Missing Migrants Project, Frontex, and Migrants File.
  - Average daily deaths are about 4.5.
  - Fatal incident locations are used to infer likely departure geography.

- Boat type:
  - Frontex for 2013–2017.
  - Polizia di Stato for 2016–2020.
  - Unsafe boats are defined progressively as:
    1. inflatable only
    2. inflatable + unknown
    3. inflatable + unknown + other

- Departure country:
  - Available in the later Polizia di Stato data (2016–2020), which supports the Libya vs Tunisia panel.

- Sea conditions:
  - ECMWF significant wave height at North African coastal points.
  - Main analysis focuses especially on Tripoli.
  - Significant wave height summarizes sea conditions relevant for crossing risk.

DESCRIPTIVE PATTERNS

- During more intense SAR periods, especially Triton II, inflatable boats become much more important.
- After the Minniti Code, inflatable use falls sharply.
- Libya-origin crossings are:
  - much larger in volume than Tunisia-origin crossings
  - much more likely to use inflatable boats
- Fatal incident locations suggest a shift in departure geography from Tunisia toward Libya starting in 2013.
- The smuggling market appears increasingly concentrated around Libya.

IMPORTANT LIMITATION

- The paper cannot directly observe daily Libyan Coast Guard interceptions.
- The authors argue this matters mainly late in the sample and does not drive the main earlier findings.

==================================================
3) METHODOLOGY
==================================================

CORE IDENTIFICATION IDEA

- The paper does not rely mainly on simple before/after comparisons.
- Reason:
  - SAR regimes change only a few times
  - they affect the whole route
  - rescues and crossings are endogenous strategic outcomes

- Instead, the paper identifies SAR indirectly through how daily crossings react to exogenous sea conditions.

THEORETICAL LOGIC

- One-boat model:
  - More SAR should increase attempts
  - and make crossings less deterred by bad weather

- Two-boat model (safe + unsafe boats):
  - If SAR makes unsafe boats feasible,
  - then crossings can become more weather-sensitive,
  - because unsafe boats are especially vulnerable to waves

- This two-boat prediction is the key moral-hazard result.

EMPIRICAL LOGIC

- If under intense SAR, and when unsafe boats are more prevalent, crossings become much more responsive to wave height,
- then the marginal crossing is likely being undertaken on an unsafe boat.
- This implies SAR increased attempted crossings partly by making fragile boats viable.

MAIN EMPIRICAL SPECIFICATION (2013–2017)

- Poisson quasi-maximum likelihood model for daily attempted crossings.
- Main regressors:
  - wave height
  - wave height × PostSAR
  - wave height × fraction unsafe boats
  - wave height × PostSAR × fraction unsafe boats
- Includes week-by-year fixed effects.
- Main coefficient of interest:
  - the triple interaction

IMPORTANT NOTATION

- PostSAR in the 2013–2017 time-series specification means the post-Mare Nostrum / intense-SAR era.
- It equals 0 during Hermes (baseline low-intensity SAR period).
- It equals 1 after October 18, 2013, when Mare Nostrum begins and rescue becomes much more far-reaching.

LATER PANEL SPECIFICATION (2016–2020)

- Daily panel by country of departure.
- Uses the Minniti Code as a sharp reduction in rescue availability near Libya.
- Compares Libya and Tunisia routes.
- Allows wave elasticity to vary by:
  - route
  - SAR availability
  - share of inflatable boats

- This is the cleaner treated-vs-comparison route mechanism test.

STRUCTURAL INTERPRETATION

- The paper uses the estimated semielasticities to infer relative safety of unsafe vs safe boats.
- This translates reduced-form weather responses into a statement about how dangerous inflatables are.

WHAT THE STATA DOFILE ADDS

- Confirms the replication mapping:
  - Table 3:
    - estimated from data_tab_main.dta
    - Poisson GLM
    - i.weekanno fixed effects
    - Newey-West HAC standard errors
    - bandwidth 28
  - Table 4:
    - ppmlhdfe on data_tab4.dta
    - high-dimensional fixed effects
  - Table 5:
    - reghdfe on daily boat-share outcomes
  - Robustness tables C3–C8:
    - alternative estimators
    - different clustering
    - lagged or max waves
    - quadratic waves
    - alternative coastal wave measures
    - threshold definitions of bad weather

==================================================
4) RESULTS
==================================================

A. MAIN TIME-SERIES RESULT

- In Table 3, the coefficient on:
  Wave Height × PostSAR × Fraction Unsafe Boat
  is negative and statistically strong across all unsafe-boat definitions:

  - about -6.546
  - about -5.450
  - about -4.168

INTERPRETATION

- When intense SAR is operating and a larger share of crossings uses unsafe boats,
  attempted crossings become much more sensitive to bad weather.
- This is exactly what the two-boat moral-hazard model predicts.

- Baseline weather deterrence:
  - a 0.10 meter increase in wave height reduces crossings on safe boats by about 8.9% to 14.6%.

B. PANEL EVIDENCE AROUND MINNITI

- In Table 4, the key coefficient is:
  From Libya × SAR × Wave Height × Fraction Inflatable Boat = -6.974

WHY THIS MATTERS

- This is very close to the main time-series estimate.
- Strong internal consistency check.

INTERPRETATION

- Before the Minniti Code, when EU/NGO rescue near Libya made inflatable crossings feasible,
  Libya-origin crossings on inflatables were especially sensitive to bad sea conditions.
- This fits the mechanism that rescue expanded the set of marginal, unsafe crossings.

- The comparison result also matters:
  - when there is no substitution toward inflatables,
    the coefficient on From Libya × SAR × Wave Height is positive,
    which matches the one-boat prediction that rescue reduces weather deterrence.

C. DIRECT EVIDENCE OF BOAT SWITCHING

- Table 5 shows that relative to Hermes:
  - Triton II is associated with a rise of about 0.606 in the inflatable share of crossings
  - shares of fishing and motor boats fall

- Figure 9 shows a sharp break around the Minniti Code:
  - inflatable share falls from about 80% before
  - to about 40% after

WHY THIS IS IMPORTANT

- This is not only a regression result.
- The mechanism is visible directly in the raw data.

D. HOW DANGEROUS ARE INFLATABLE BOATS?

- Using the model’s structural mapping and estimated semielasticities,
  the paper infers a very small relative safety parameter for unsafe boats.
- For plausible price ratios, theta is around 5% to 10%.

INTERPRETATION

- Inflatables are an order of magnitude less safe than sturdier alternatives.

- The abstract summarizes the same point more starkly:
  - inflatables are about 20 times more dangerous than sturdy wooden boats.

- Exact magnitude depends on the comparison boat type,
  but the qualitative conclusion is very clear:
  the marginal boats enabled under intense SAR are far more dangerous.

E. POLICY CONCLUSION

- The paper does NOT simply argue that SAR should be abolished.
- The authors argue SAR likely increased aggregate migrant welfare,
  because it allowed more people to afford the journey.
- But it also generated strong strategic responses:
  - more marginal crossings
  - more fragile boats
  - partial offset of intended safety gains

- Their constructive policy suggestion:
  - restrict the supply of inflatable rafts
  - expand legal alternatives
  - do not rely on rescue alone

==================================================
5) IDENTIFICATION CRITIQUE / HOW CONVINCING IS THE CAUSAL CLAIM?
==================================================

CORE CONCERN

- A simple post-2013 comparison would be unconvincing.
- Many other things changed during the migration crisis:
  - conflict and instability
  - migrant composition
  - smuggling-market organization
  - policy changes in Europe and North Africa
  - NGO presence
  - broader crisis dynamics

- Also, SAR itself is endogenous:
  - more crossings can trigger more SAR
  - more deaths can trigger more SAR
  - so SAR is partly a consequence of crossing attempts, not just a cause

IMPORTANT HONEST ASSESSMENT

- The paper does NOT prove in a clean experimental sense that intense SAR alone caused the observed changes.
- Its evidence is stronger than a naive before/after design, but still rests on assumptions.

WHAT THE PAPER TRIES TO IDENTIFY

- The paper does not mainly identify SAR from levels.
- Instead, it identifies SAR indirectly from changes in the weather elasticity of crossings.

- The claim is NOT:
  - “crossings rose after SAR, therefore SAR caused them.”

- The claim IS:
  - “when rescue becomes more extensive, and especially when unsafe boats are used,
     crossings react differently to exogenous wave conditions;
     under the model, that pattern is consistent with SAR-induced moral hazard.”

WHY THIS HELPS

- Wave height is plausibly exogenous at the daily level.
- In the main 2013–2017 design, the model includes week-by-year fixed effects.
- So identification comes from day-to-day variation in waves within narrow time windows,
  not from broad annual shifts in the migration crisis.

- This absorbs many slow-moving confounders:
  - seasonal demand
  - annual crisis trends
  - low-frequency policy and market changes

- Therefore, a confounder would need to do something quite specific:
  - it would have to change the daily slope of crossings with respect to wave height
    exactly when SAR changes,
    in a way that mimics the model’s predictions.

WHY THE LIBYA VS TUNISIA DESIGN IS STRONGER

- The 2016–2020 panel around the Minniti Code is more convincing than the pure time-series design.
- It compares:
  - Libya-origin crossings (more exposed to NGO rescue near Libya)
  - Tunisia-origin crossings (less exposed comparison route)

- This is better because it is not only before/after.
- It is also differential exposure across routes.

- The main interaction is specific to:
  - Libya × SAR × Wave Height × Inflatable share

- That makes the interpretation more credible than a simple aggregate post dummy.

WHAT SUPPORTS THEIR INTERPRETATION

- Robustness to:
  - OLS instead of Poisson
  - different inference choices
  - lagged or max waves
  - quadratic waves
  - alternative sea-condition measures
  - threshold “bad weather” versions

- Placebo / randomization logic:
  - replacing current waves with distant lagged waves should not predict current crossings
  - the true coefficients are unusually strong relative to those placebo distributions

- Internal consistency:
  - the Libya–Tunisia estimate is very close to the main time-series estimate

WHAT STILL LIMITS THE PAPER

- Very few major SAR-regime changes
- SAR regimes overlap with many other institutional shifts
- Boat-share measures are themselves endogenous
- No direct daily observation of Libyan Coast Guard interceptions
- Strongest empirical evidence is about behavior and mechanism,
  not a pristine reduced-form estimate of total deaths caused by SAR

BEST CRITICAL SUMMARY

- The paper does not cleanly prove that intense SAR alone caused all observed changes.
- What it identifies more credibly is that the weather responsiveness of crossings changed across SAR regimes
  in a way consistent with SAR-induced behavioral adaptation and substitution toward unsafe boats.
- The causal claim is therefore best read as:
  evidence consistent with moral hazard,
  stronger on mechanism than on full causal proof.

==================================================
6) CONNECTING METHODOLOGY TO RESULTS
==================================================

KEY DEEP LOGIC

- The paper’s evidence is not mainly level-based.
- It does NOT say:
  “crossings rose after SAR, therefore SAR caused them.”

- Instead it says:
  when rescue becomes more extensive, and especially when unsafe boats are used,
  crossings react differently to exogenous sea conditions.

- This change in weather elasticity is the empirical fingerprint of moral hazard in their model.

HOW THE RESULTS FIT TOGETHER

- Table 3:
  broad time-series version of the mechanism
- Table 4:
  cleaner route-specific version using the Minniti shock
- Table 5 and Figure 9:
  direct evidence that smugglers switched boat types
- Figure 8:
  translates the estimated semielasticities into implied relative danger of inflatable boats

SO THE STRONGEST CLAIM IS NOT:

- “we measured the exact net death effect of SAR”

THE STRONGEST CLAIM IS:

- SAR changed strategic behavior in exactly the way a moral-hazard model predicts:
  - more marginal crossings
  - more fragile boats
  - partial offset of intended safety gains

==================================================
7) BOTTOM LINE
==================================================

- The paper argues that extensive SAR near Libya had unintended consequences.
- By making rescue more likely, it made crossings on unsafe inflatable boats more feasible.
- This increased attempted crossings and changed the composition of boats used.
- Because inflatable boats are much more dangerous, part of SAR’s intended safety effect was offset.

MOST IMPORTANT TAKEAWAY

- The core contribution is the combination of:
  - theory of moral hazard
  - identification through weather responsiveness
  - direct evidence of substitution toward inflatable boats

MAIN STRENGTH

- Clever use of exogenous sea conditions to identify strategic behavioral responses.

MAIN LIMITATION

- The paper does not directly estimate the full unconditional mortality effect of SAR.
- It mainly identifies how SAR changes behavior and inferred risk through boat composition and weather sensitivity.
