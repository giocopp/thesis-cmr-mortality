Estimand Definitions and Choices

Author

Giorgio Coppola

Published

March 17, 2026

Purpose

This document defines the theoretical estimand, empirical estimand, and estimation strategy for the thesis, following the framework of Lundberg, Johnson, and Stewart (2021). Every quantitative study must answer: what is your estimand? The estimand is the target quantity — defined outside any statistical model — that exists whether or not we have data.

The framework requires three explicit steps:

  1. Set the theoretical estimand (by argument connecting to theory)
  2. Link to the empirical estimand (by assumption, using the DAG)
  3. Learn from data (by estimation strategy)

This document records how each step applies to the thesis. It complements dag_identification.qmd (DAG specification and identification) and weather_variable_strategy.qmd (weather covariate selection).

1. Theoretical estimand

1.1 Unit-specific quantity

For each incident \(i\) on the Central Mediterranean Route, the unit-specific quantity is:

\[\tau_i = E[\text{deaths}_i \mid \text{post-MoU}] - E[\text{deaths}_i \mid \text{pre-MoU}]\]

interpreted as: the change in expected deaths per incident attributable to the system changes induced by the 2017 EU–Libya Memorandum of Understanding (SAR withdrawal, boat degradation, smuggler network disruption), under the weather conditions \(w_i\) actually experienced at incident \(i\).

This is a causal unit-specific quantity: it involves counterfactual potential outcomes (what would have happened to incident \(i\) under the pre-MoU regime?).

NoteWhy incidents, not crossings?

The IOM Missing Migrants Project records incidents (events where deaths or disappearances occurred, or were narrowly avoided). An incident is a subset of crossings — many crossings produce no recorded incident. The unit-specific quantity is therefore defined over observed incidents, not all crossings.

This means we estimate the change in severity conditional on an incident occurring. This is appropriate: it captures whether each distress event became more lethal post-MoU, which is the operationally meaningful measure of danger at sea.

1.2 Target population

The target population is all actual incidents on the Central Mediterranean Route during the study period.

This choice has an important implication: we estimate an effect among those who crossed (and experienced an incident). In the language of causal inference, this is an Average Treatment Effect on the Treated (ATT), where “treated” means “exposed to the post-MoU regime.”

ImportantWhy ATT, not ATE?

The target population is actual crossings, not the broader set of “all potential crossers including those deterred by the MoU.” A deterred crossing is not a crossing — it has no coordinates, no weather conditions, no boat, no outcome. The unit-specific quantity (mortality risk of a crossing) is undefined for a non-event.

This parallels Pager (2003), where the target population is “the pool of applications actually sent by two testers in the Milwaukee area” — not all hypothetical applications that could have been sent. Pager does not estimate the effect for applications that were never submitted.

From a policy perspective, the ATT answers the right question: did the route become more dangerous for those who cross it? If the goal were to protect all potential migrants (including those deterred), the policy response would be to open legal pathways — a valid but entirely different question, outside the scope of this thesis.

1.3 The MoU may have changed who crosses

The composition of crossers likely changed post-MoU. Deterrence may have filtered out the most cautious or risk-averse, leaving a population that is more desperate or less able to time departures safely. This does not invalidate the ATT — it is part of what the policy did. The ATT post-MoU answers: “for the people who actually cross under the new regime, how dangerous is it?”

This compositional change is relevant for interpretation: the ATT post-MoU is defined over a potentially different subpopulation than the ATT pre-MoU. The triple-difference design (comparing CMR to control routes across periods) partially accounts for secular changes in migrant composition, but route-specific selection remains a channel through which the MoU operates.

1.4 Linking the theoretical estimand to theory

The thesis asks: did the 2017 EU–Libya MoU make the Central Mediterranean Route more dangerous? “Danger” is operationalized as per-incident severity — the expected number of deaths conditional on an incident.

The theoretical estimand connects to the theory of policy externalization:

  • The MoU externalized SAR to the Libyan Coast Guard (Zambiasi & Albarosa 2025), degrading rescue capacity.
  • Smuggler networks responded by shifting to cheaper, more dangerous boats (Deiana et al. 2024; Hoffmann Pham & Komiyama 2024).
  • These system changes increased vulnerability at sea.

The unit-specific quantity (change in deaths per incident) captures the net effect of all these channels simultaneously.

1.5 Why the total effect, not decomposition?

The primary estimand captures the total average effect across all channels of risk that the MoU changed (SAR, boats, smuggler behavior). We do not decompose the effect into individual channels because:

  1. Data limitations: SAR capacity and boat type are only partially observed. Decomposition requires fully observed mediators (see dag_identification.qmd Sections 6 and 10).
  2. Policy relevance: The MoU was a package — SAR withdrawal, coast guard cooperation, border tightening. The policy-relevant question is whether the package increased danger, not which component did so.

Decomposition via mediation analysis (NDE/NIE for the SAR and boats pathways) and LATE estimation (using the MoU as an instrument for SAR withdrawal) are valuable extensions, proposed for future work contingent on obtaining Frontex JORA boat-type data and NGO fleet SAR proxy data. The current analysis estimates the ITT: the total effect of being in the post-MoU period.

2. Empirical estimand

2.1 From theoretical to empirical: the interaction design

The theoretical estimand involves unobservable counterfactuals (what would deaths have been under the pre-MoU regime?). The empirical estimand must be a function of observable data only.

The identification strategy exploits exogenous variation in sea conditions: if the MoU degraded the safety infrastructure that buffered crossers against bad weather, then the relationship between weather and deaths should change post-MoU on the CMR relative to control routes.

The empirical estimand is the Weather \(\times\) Post-MoU \(\times\) CMR triple interaction, which captures the differential change in the weather–mortality gradient on the CMR versus control routes, before versus after the MoU.

In the negative binomial specification with log link:

\[\log \mu_i = \alpha_g + \lambda_t + \beta_3 (\text{SWH}_i \times \text{Post}_t \times \text{CMR}_r) + \text{lower-order interactions} + \mathbf{X}_i'\gamma\]

the empirical estimand is \(\exp(\beta_3)\):

For each additional 1m of significant wave height, the expected number of deaths per incident on the CMR post-MoU is \(\exp(\beta_3)\) times what it would be based on the pre-MoU weather–mortality gradient (or the gradient on control routes).

2.2 The causal interaction interpretation

The Weather \(\times\) Post interaction is a causal interaction, not merely moderation. Following the distinction in Lecture 4 (slides 22–24):

  • Moderation: “How does the MoU’s effect on deaths vary across weather conditions?” Weather is a passive context variable. Only the MoU needs to be exogenous.
  • Causal interaction: “Does the joint exposure to bad weather AND the post-MoU regime produce more deaths than the sum of each individual effect?” Both weather and the MoU are “treatments.” Both must be exogenous.

The causal interaction interpretation is valid because both variables satisfy the exogeneity requirement:

  • Weather: exogenous by physics, conditional on temporal and spatial fixed effects (see dag_identification.qmd Section 3).
  • MoU: exogenous by DAG — no parents, no backdoor paths (see dag_identification.qmd Section 2).

The causal interaction reveals synergy: the MoU removed the safety infrastructure that previously buffered crossers against environmental hazards.

  • Bad weather alone (pre-MoU): SAR rescued distressed boats. Weather was dangerous, but the system mitigated it.
  • MoU alone (calm seas): even degraded boats and reduced SAR may not be lethal in benign conditions.
  • Bad weather + post-MoU: the combination is lethal. \(\beta_3 > 0\) means super-additivity — the joint effect exceeds the sum of individual effects.

The policy implication is sharper under the causal interaction framing: it is not generically “the MoU is dangerous” but specifically “the MoU amplified the lethality of environmental hazards by removing protective infrastructure.” This points to restoring SAR capacity as the targeted policy response.

2.3 Identification assumptions

The empirical estimand equals the theoretical estimand under the following assumptions (verified formally in dag_identification.qmd):

Assumption 1: Weather exogeneity conditional on FE. Sea conditions, conditional on temporal and spatial fixed effects, are not caused by any variable that also causes deaths. This is very strong: atmospheric and oceanic physics determine weather, not policy or human behavior. DAG verification: the only backdoor path from Weather to Deaths passes through FE (Weather \(\leftarrow\) FE \(\rightarrow\) Deaths); conditioning on FE blocks it (dag_identification.qmd Section 3).

Assumption 2: MoU exogeneity. The MoU has no parents in the DAG. No backdoor paths from MoU to Deaths exist. adjustmentSets(dag, "MoU", "Deaths") returns the empty set (dag_identification.qmd Section 3).

Assumption 3: Parallel gradient trends. Absent the MoU, the weather–mortality gradient (how weather affects deaths per incident) would have evolved similarly on the CMR and control routes. This is the parallel trends assumption for the triple-difference, applied to the slope rather than levels. Testable in the pre-period: the weather–mortality gradient should be stable and similar across routes before Feb 2017.

Assumption 4: Exclusion restriction for the interaction. The change in \(\beta(\text{Weather} \rightarrow \text{Deaths})\) post-MoU on the CMR operates through the vulnerability mechanism (SAR, boats, conditions), not through some other channel that coincidentally changed at the same time on the CMR only. The DAG encodes this: no direct Weather \(\rightarrow\) Deaths edge bypassing Vulnerability. Defended by mechanism — all pathways from weather to death (capsizing, drift, hypothermia, failed rescue) operate through crossing vulnerability. See dag_identification.qmd Section 16 (potential missing edges table) for discussion.

Assumption 5: SUTVA. One incident’s outcome does not affect another’s. Potentially violated if a mass-casualty event overwhelms SAR capacity for subsequent incidents. Acknowledged as a minor limitation.

Assumption 6: No anticipation. The weather–mortality gradient did not shift before the MoU on the CMR. Testable with placebo treatment dates in the pre-period.

2.4 Fixed effects structure: an open modeling decision

The DAG’s “FE” node is an abstraction representing seasonal and spatial confounding. For Model A (event-level), the specific temporal FE granularity is a modeling decision with trade-offs:

FE structure What it absorbs Trade-off
Month-of-year + grid Seasonality + spatial heterogeneity Leaves year-to-year trends uncontrolled
Calendar month + grid Seasonality + time trends + spatial Absorbs more variation; may reduce power for the interaction
Week-of-year + grid Finer seasonality More FE parameters; better controls for within-season variation
Year \(\times\) month-of-year + grid Most flexible Risk of overfitting; absorbs maximum confounding but leaves less identifying variation

Literature precedents:

  • Deiana et al. (2024): week-by-year FE
  • Camarena et al. (2020): month + day-of-week FE
  • Zambiasi & Albarosa (2025): month + quadrant FE + quadrant-specific trends

The identifying variation comes from within-FE weather variation interacted with the Post dummy and route indicator. Finer temporal FE absorb more confounding but reduce residual variation for the interaction. The analysis should test multiple FE structures and report sensitivity of \(\beta_3\). Robustness across FE specifications strengthens the result.

Grid FE are determined by the ERA5 spatial resolution (0.25 degree). Incidents are assigned to grid cells based on geocoded coordinates.

3. Estimation strategy

3.1 Model A: event-level negative binomial

The primary specification is:

\[\text{deaths}_i \sim \text{NegBin}(\mu_i), \quad \log \mu_i = \alpha_g + \lambda_t + \beta_3 (\text{SWH}_i \times \text{Post}_t \times \text{CMR}_r) + \text{lower-order terms} + \mathbf{X}_i'\gamma\]

where:

  • \(i\) indexes individual incidents (IOM MMP records)
  • \(\alpha_g\) is a grid fixed effect (spatial)
  • \(\lambda_t\) is a temporal fixed effect (granularity TBD, see Section 2.4)
  • \(\text{SWH}_i\) is the significant wave height at the incident’s coordinates and date (from ERA5)
  • \(\text{Post}_t\) is a binary indicator for the post-MoU period (Feb 2017 onward)
  • \(\text{CMR}_r\) is a binary indicator for the Central Mediterranean Route
  • \(\mathbf{X}_i\) includes additional weather controls (wind speed, SST, currents) and any available covariates

Why negative binomial? Deaths per incident are non-negative counts with overdispersion: ~23% zero-death incidents on the CMR and a long right tail (mass-casualty shipwrecks). The Poisson assumption (mean = variance) is almost certainly violated. The negative binomial adds a dispersion parameter that allows variance \(>\) mean. See dag_identification.qmd Section 14 for diagnostics and alternatives considered.

3.2 Interpreting \(\exp(\beta_3)\): the estimand on the

multiplicative scale

In the NegBin with log link, \(\beta_3\) is a log-multiplicative interaction:

  • Pre-MoU on CMR: a 1m increase in SWH multiplies expected deaths per incident by \(\exp(\beta_1)\)
  • Post-MoU on CMR: a 1m increase in SWH multiplies expected deaths per incident by \(\exp(\beta_1 + \beta_3)\)
  • The ratio: \(\exp(\beta_3)\) — how much more (multiplicatively) a unit SWH increase raises expected deaths post-MoU vs. pre-MoU, relative to control routes

\(\exp(\beta_3)\) is directly the estimand on the multiplicative scale. It is constant — it does not depend on covariate values. It is an incidence rate ratio of ratios: the ratio of the post-MoU weather IRR to the pre-MoU weather IRR (differenced across CMR vs. controls).

Example: if \(\hat{\beta}_3 = 0.3\), then \(\exp(0.3) \approx 1.35\), meaning a 1m SWH increase raises expected deaths per incident by 35% more on the CMR post-MoU than the same increase did pre-MoU or on control routes.

3.3 When to use average marginal effects

On the additive (count) scale, the marginal effect of weather depends on the baseline expected count \(\mu_i\), which varies across observations:

\[\frac{\partial \mu_i}{\partial \text{SWH}} = \mu_i \cdot (\beta_1 + \beta_3 \cdot \text{Post}_t \cdot \text{CMR}_r)\]

To report a single summary on the count scale (“how many additional expected deaths does a 1m SWH increase cause post-MoU vs. pre-MoU?”), average over the covariate distribution using marginaleffects::avg_comparisons().

Recommendation for the paper:

  1. Primary: report \(\exp(\beta_3)\) as the main result (multiplicative, constant, directly interpretable).
  2. Supplementary: report AME from marginaleffects at meaningful SWH values (median, 75th percentile, storm threshold) for policy communication, since counts are more intuitive than ratios for a non-technical audience.

3.4 Complementary specifications

Model Estimand Data level Crossings needed? What \(\beta_3\) captures
A (primary) \(E[\text{deaths} \mid \text{incident}, w, t]\) Event No Severity: did weather kill more per incident post-MoU?
Monthly counts \(E[\text{deaths}_t \mid w, t]\) Monthly No Total effect: more deaths in bad-weather months post-MoU? (mixes volume + danger)
Crossings diagnostic \(E[\text{crossings}_t \mid w, t]\) Monthly As outcome Volume channel: did weather’s deterrence of crossings change post-MoU?
D (exploratory) \(\text{deaths}_t / \text{crossings}_t\) Monthly Yes \(\rightarrow\) collider Per-crossing danger, with acknowledged collider bias

Model A avoids conditioning on crossings (a post-treatment variable) and therefore avoids the Kronmal collider bias documented in dag_identification.qmd Section 5. The crossings diagnostic (dag_identification.qmd Section 12) helps disentangle volume from danger without introducing collider bias, since crossings appear as an outcome rather than a conditioning variable.

3.5 Robustness specifications

  • Poisson QMLE: consistent under heteroskedasticity even if variance function is misspecified (Deiana et al. 2024 use this for crossing counts)
  • DML (partially linear): doubly-robust machine learning, flexibly controls for weather and covariates
  • Causal forest: treatment effect heterogeneity across geographic subgroups
  • Lee bounds: sensitivity to potential selection on unobservables

If \(\hat{\beta}_3\) is similar across NegBin, Poisson, and DML, the functional form choice is not driving results.

4. The ITT interpretation

4.1 What we estimate is an ITT

The post-MoU dummy is an assignment to a policy regime, not a precise measure of treatment receipt. Not all post-MoU crossings faced equally degraded conditions: some still had NGO SAR coverage, some used wooden boats, enforcement varied over time. The Weather \(\times\) Post \(\times\) CMR interaction therefore estimates an intention-to-treat (ITT) effect: the total effect of being in the post-MoU period on the weather–mortality gradient.

This parallels the COVID mask experiment from Lecture 4: the ideal estimand was the ATE of individual mask-wearing, but non-compliance and interference forced a shift to the ITT (effect of being in a treated village). Similarly, the ideal estimand might be the effect of actual SAR withdrawal on the weather–mortality gradient, but imperfect and gradual implementation means we estimate the ITT of the policy period.

4.2 The ITT is the right estimand for this thesis

The ITT answers the policy question directly: what happened to the weather–mortality relationship after the MoU? This encompasses all channels through which the MoU operated (SAR, boats, smuggler behavior, deterrence), without requiring data on which specific channel affected each crossing.

4.3 Extensions requiring additional data

With additional data, future work could sharpen the estimate:

  • LATE: if SAR capacity data were available as a continuous variable, the MoU could serve as an instrument for SAR withdrawal. The LATE would estimate the effect of actual SAR degradation among crossings where SAR was actually withdrawn (“compliers”). This isolates the SAR channel.
  • Mediation (NDE/NIE): with SAR and boat-type data, the total MoU effect could be decomposed into the SAR pathway (MoU \(\rightarrow\) SAR \(\rightarrow\) Vulnerability \(\rightarrow\) Deaths) and the boats pathway (MoU \(\rightarrow\) Boats \(\rightarrow\) Vulnerability \(\rightarrow\) Deaths). This answers: was it SAR withdrawal or boat degradation that killed people? Each implies different policy responses.
  • CDE: the Controlled Direct Effect of the MoU holding SAR fixed at pre-MoU levels would isolate the boats channel.

These are proposed as extensions in the paper, contingent on obtaining Frontex JORA boat-type data and NGO fleet SAR proxy data.

5. Heterogeneous treatment effects

5.1 Primary interest: average total effect

The primary estimand is the average \(\exp(\beta_3)\) across all incidents — the net change in the weather–mortality gradient, averaging over all channels without decomposition. This answers: on average, how much more lethal did bad weather become after the MoU?

5.2 Geographic heterogeneity (feasible)

CATE by geographic subgroups is estimable with current data. Different zones along the CMR may show different vulnerability changes:

  • Incidents close to Libya vs. close to Italy (distance from SAR bases)
  • Western CMR (Tunisia–Sardinia) vs. central CMR (Libya–Lampedusa) vs. eastern CMR (Libya–Crete)
  • Grid-cell-level variation in incident density and mortality

This connects to the transportability concept from Lecture 4 (Cinelli & Bareinboim 2019): geographic subgroups are effect modifiers that may change the magnitude of \(\beta_3\). Estimating subgroup effects (\(\beta_3\) by zone) allows reconstructing the aggregate effect for different spatial compositions of crossings.

5.3 Channel-specific heterogeneity (requires data)

CATE by boat type (inflatable vs. wooden) or SAR availability (NGO present vs. absent) requires data not yet available:

  • Frontex JORA: boat-type information (requested, pending)
  • NGO fleet data: SAR proxy for rescue availability (not yet sourced)

If obtained, these would allow estimating whether the weather–mortality gradient changed more for inflatable boats (consistent with the boats channel) or more in SAR-absent zones (consistent with the SAR channel).

6. Target population and generalizability

6.1 What the results speak to

The target population is CMR incidents during the study period. Generalizing to other contexts requires assessing whether effect modifiers have similar distributions (Lecture 4: Cinelli & Bareinboim 2019; what matters for transportability is not propensity to be treated, but the distribution of effect modifiers).

Key effect modifiers for the weather–mortality relationship:

  • Boat type and quality
  • Distance from coast / SAR bases
  • SAR availability and response time
  • Crossing route geometry (distance, exposure to open sea)
  • Migrant demographics and physical condition

6.2 Transportability to other routes

The triple-difference uses EMR, WMR, and WAAR as control routes. These routes differ substantially in effect modifier distributions:

  • EMR (Eastern Mediterranean): shorter crossings, Turkish SAR more functional, different boat types
  • WMR (Western Mediterranean): shorter crossings, different SAR regime (Spanish/Moroccan)
  • WAAR (Atlantic/West Africa): very long crossings, minimal SAR, different boat technology

The control routes serve to absorb common time trends, not to claim that effects would be identical across routes. The results speak to the CMR specifically.

6.3 Transportability to future periods

The results inform future policy if the MoU \(\rightarrow\) SAR/Boats mechanism remains operative. If the policy regime changes (e.g., restored NGO SAR, new EU agreements), the effect modifier distribution changes and the estimate may not transport. This is a standard limitation of any policy evaluation.
